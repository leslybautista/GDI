6.1 · GDI Research

The Next GDI Generation & Scientific GDI

GDI Species — The Evolving Landscape

GDI today is not a single monolithic concept. The lecture identifies four coexisting GDI species, each with distinct drivers, stakeholders, and technical requirements:

aGDI

Administrative GDI. Driven by governments, INSPIRE, GDI-DE. Formal, regulated, authoritative data (ALKIS, ATKIS).

sGDI

Scientific GDI. Research communities sharing simulation data, models, observations. FAIR data, DOIs, reproducibility.

cMS

Commercial Map Services. Google Maps, HERE, Esri. Driven by market, proprietary APIs, business models.

vGI

Volunteered Geographic Information. OpenStreetMap, citizen science. Crowdsourced, bi-directional, open.

The "next generation GDI" vision sees these species converging alongside emerging domains: GEOSS (Global Earth Observation System of Systems), Digital Earth, GeoSensor Web, GeoSim Web, and Research Infrastructures — all integrated in an interoperable GDI 2.0/3.0/4.0.

The Next GDI Generation — Vision

Four key capabilities distinguish the next generation from today's GDI:

Bi-directional Transactions, crowdsourcing, VGI — not just data consumption but data contribution (chapters 3.1, 5.5)
Ad-hoc Fusion On-the-fly geodata fusion across heterogeneous sources without pre-arrangement (chapter 5.5)
Shared Geoprocessing Sharing processing capabilities for ad-hoc and reproducible analysis — WPS, moving code (chapter 6.1)
Real-time + Simulation Integration of near-real-time sensor observations and simulation results (chapter 6.2)
Semantic Interop. Machine-readable meaning, not just syntax — ontologies, thesauri, auto-discovery (chapters 5.7, 6.3)

Scientific GDI (sGDI)

Research communities increasingly build and use GDI for scientific purposes — sharing data, simulations, and analysis methods across disciplines. Examples: the IPCC Interactive Atlas (OGC-compliant WMS for climate visualization), GLUES GDI for global land management research, COLABIS air quality information systems.

Scientific GDI builds on the same OGC/ISO standards as administrative GDI — WMS, WFS, WCS — but has additional requirements that are not yet well addressed by existing standards.

Challenges Specific to Scientific GDI

ChallengeWhy it mattersStatus
Time & Scenarios in Metadata/WMS Scientific data is inherently time-variant (climate projections, simulation scenarios with future branch time). Standard metadata and WMS were designed for static, administrative data. Technically possible to add time dimensions to WMS; rarely implemented in practice
Lineage / Provenance Scientists need to trace how a dataset was derived — which model, which parameters, which source data — to assess usability and reproduce results. Linked to reproducible research. ISO 19115 supports lineage only unidirectionally; bi-directional links (data → services that used it) are not defined
Scenario / simulation metadata vocabulary No common structures, taxonomies, or vocabularies to describe simulation assumptions, temporal resolution, uncertainties, sensitivities Open research problem; no standard yet
UUIDs and DOIs Globally accepted persistent identifiers for metadata records, data, services, and software/applications are needed to enable reliable cross-linking and citation DOIs are available via DataCite, PANGAEA; not yet systematically applied to all GI resources
Automated metadata generation Current GIS applications do not automatically log full processing history; metadata creation remains mostly manual Active research; COLABIS project worked on closing the "metadata gap"
Privacy and data protection Health, social, and environmental data often contains sensitive personal information; automated compliance checking is needed to allow easier online access Requires strict formalization of privacy rules; "intra muros" computation (aggregated results only)
Incentives for data publication Scientists are rewarded for paper publications, not data publications. Requires acknowledging data & software publications similar to journal articles (ISI citation index equivalent) Emerging: DataCite, PANGAEA, ORCID; cultural change needed in academia
The core tension: Scientific data is dynamic, uncertain, scenario-based, and process-dependent. Existing GDI metadata standards (ISO 19115) were designed for static, authoritative administrative datasets. Adapting them to scientific workflows is an ongoing research challenge.

NFDI — National Research Data Infrastructure (Germany)

The Nationale Forschungsdateninfrastruktur (NFDI) is a German federal initiative (2018 coalition agreement) to systematize scientific data holdings and ensure sustainable, FAIR access. Funded at 90 million €/year for 10+ years through the Bund-Länder programme; up to 30 domain-specific consortia.

Built on FAIR Principles (go-fair.org):

F

Findable — persistent identifiers, rich metadata, indexed in searchable resources

A

Accessible — retrievable by identifier, open standardized protocol, metadata accessible even when data is not

I

Interoperable — formal shared knowledge representation, FAIR vocabularies, qualified references to other data

R

Reusable — clear usage licenses, provenance, domain-relevant community standards

NFDI4Earth

The Earth System Sciences consortium within NFDI (nfdi4earth.de). Covers: geosphere, atmosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, cryosphere, and anthroposphere. Addresses topics from local processes to global challenges — climate change, land-use change, natural hazards, water scarcity.

Problems NFDI4Earth addresses:

  • Over 100 data services related to Earth System Sciences, but only few are suitable/interoperable
  • Highly diverse data qualities: different dimensions, semantics, scales, temporal/spatial resolutions, provenance
  • Different data cultures across sub-disciplines; uneven RDM expertise
  • Incomplete support along the full data lifecycle
  • Lack of platforms and tools for joint interpretation of heterogeneous, decentralized data
6.2 · Distributed Geoprocessing

Distributed Geoprocessing — WPS & Beyond

Why Distributed Geoprocessing?

GDI as described in Chapters 4–5 provides distributed data (WFS, WCS) and visualization (WMS). But many real-world tasks require not just accessing data but also processing it — interpolations, aggregations, spatial joins, classification, routing. The next generation GDI needs to share processing capabilities as interoperably as it shares data.

Three escalating levels of sharing geoprocessing:

Level 1 — Tools Sharing desktop GIS tools (ArcGIS, QGIS, GRASS). Algorithms stay proprietary; no interoperability. Established, but silo-based.
Level 2 — Functions Sharing geoprocessing functions as web services (WPS). Interoperable offering and use of individual geoprocessing operations. Current state of practice.
Level 3 — Logic Sharing geoprocessing algorithms / logic in a formalized, re-deployable way across platforms. Requires agreed formalisms and exchange mechanisms. Currently a research goal.

Web Processing Service (WPS)

OGC WPS 2.0.0 (2014, OGC 14-065) defines a generic interface for offering any geoprocessing function as a web service. It is technology-neutral — any function (buffer, interpolation, routing, classification) can be wrapped and offered through the same standard interface. WPS is stateless and supports both synchronous and asynchronous execution.

WPS Core Concepts

Process

A computational function with a unique URI identifier. Has defined Inputs (0 or more, each with min/maxOccurs) and Outputs (1 or more). Data types: literal, bbox, complex (extendable for any format).

Job

A single execution of a process. Has a lifecycle: created → running → succeeded/failed. Each job has a unique JobID. Clients can monitor job status asynchronously via GetStatus.

Data Types

Literal: simple values (numbers, strings). BoundingBox: spatial extent. Complex: any structured format — GML, GeoJSON, raster formats, NetCDF. Complex type is extendable to accommodate new formats.

WPS Operations Sequence

GetCapabilities
Returns service metadata — which processes are offered, what protocols are supported, version information.
DescribeProcess
Returns the full description of a specific process: its inputs (identifiers, data types, cardinality), outputs (identifiers, data types), and free-text description. The client must call this before Execute to know what to send.
Execute
Submits a process for execution with the specified input data. Can be synchronous (server blocks and returns result) or asynchronous (server returns a JobID; client polls with GetStatus/GetResult). Data can be sent by value (inline) or by reference (URL).

Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Execution

Synchronous (a)

Client sends Execute request → Server runs job → Server returns result directly in the response. Simple but blocks the client connection for the full duration. Suitable for fast processes.

Asynchronous (b)

Client sends Execute → Server creates job, immediately returns StatusInfo with JobID → Client polls GetStatus(JobID) at intervals → When finished, client calls GetResult(JobID). Required for long-running processes (interpolation, simulation).

Data Transfer: By Value vs. By Reference

Call by value / Response by value

Input data is embedded inline in the Execute request. Result is embedded inline in the response. Simple but impractical for large datasets — transmits data twice over the wire.

Call by reference / Response by reference

Execute request contains a URL reference to input data (e.g., a WFS endpoint). Server fetches data directly from the source. Result is stored on the server; response contains a URL reference. Client queries the result data separately. Efficient for large datasets already in GDI.

Call by reference is the GDI-native pattern: A WPS processing pipeline looks like — WFS (source data) → URL reference in Execute → WPS processes it → result URL → WCS or WFS serves result → WMS visualizes. No data is ever transmitted unnecessarily; everything flows through service references.

WPS Profiles — Adding Semantics

The base WPS interface is purely syntactic — it says nothing about what a process means. WPS profiles add semantic descriptions: what kind of spatial operation does this process perform? What are the conceptual semantics of its inputs and outputs? This is where WPS links forward to Geo Semantics (chapter 6.4) and the challenges of semantic service chaining.

Example: Forest Fire Hazard Assessment (ORCHESTRA Project)

EU Research Project ORCHESTRA (2004–08) — pre-cursor to INSPIRE. Implemented a full WPS-based distributed geoprocessing pipeline for European forest fire risk assessment:

WFS (MS1, MS2: fire records, different schemas)
→

→
WPS: Join & Aggregation by NUTS admin units → fire frequency
→

→

This scenario showed that interoperable, distributed geoprocessing across national borders and different data formats is possible with WPS + WFS + WMS — but requires significant upfront work on schema harmonization and process design.

OGC API – Processes (REST equivalent of WPS)

The modern REST/OpenAPI replacement for WPS. Part 1 Core published December 2021. Architecture:

Moving Data vs. Moving Code — The Fundamental Tension

The current WPS paradigm follows a data-driven model: data is transported to the processing function. This approach has fundamental limits at scale.

Data-driven (current WPS)Algorithm-driven (tightly coupled)Algorithm-driven (loosely coupled)
Principle Data is sequentially transported to WPS and processed Code is deployed directly on the data source (like distributed databases) Code is deployed on any platform, can access varying data sources
Advantages Flexible; current state of the art (WFS → WPS → WMS) Reduces needless data transfer; partly works with protected data Very flexible; links well with cloud computing; allows reuse on different platforms; works with protected data
Disadvantages Potentially huge data transfer (does not scale); not applicable on protected data Less flexible; tightly coupled to specific data sources and databases Not yet available; lacks common geoprocessing languages
Status Established Partial implementations (distributed databases) Research goal
Moving Code Architecture (Müller, Bernard, Kadner 2013): A Geoprocessing Repository stores published stable algorithms. Developers publish algorithms; users find algorithms in the repository and bind them to an algorithm feed. A WPS Instance deploys the algorithm from the feed and connects it to the relevant data servers (WFS, WCS, SOS). This separates the algorithm from the data source — the same algorithm can run on different data.

Open Challenges for Moving Code

  • Functionality: How to describe the algorithm semantically? What is its interface? → WPS Profiles, taxonomies, geo-algebras
  • Platform: Which software components and data sources must be in place? → URIs, registries
  • Infrastructure: Which compute resources (memory, cores, storage) are required? → Cloud standards (OCCI)
  • Legal: Which licenses and copyrights apply to both code and data? → Creative Commons, formal licensing

Conclusion: Distributed Geoprocessing

  • WPS is the current basis for interoperable geoprocessing in GDI
  • Open research challenges: moving geocode (instead of moving data), formalizing geooperators with shared semantics, composing distributed geoprocessing workflows
  • Strong links to reproducible research — sharing the full processing chain, not just the result
6.3 · Interoperable Geosimulations & Geo Sensor Web

Interoperable Geosimulations & Geo Sensor Web

The Problem Space — Two Motivating Scenarios

Scenario 1: Environmental Planning

A planned construction site in Fictivevalley. Questions: Which effects does the planned site have on nocturnal air flows? Where and when does it increase air pollution? Requires: simulation of scenarios + management, analysis, and assessment of spatio-temporal information (pre- and post-processing). Tools needed: GIS + simulation systems working together.

Scenario 2: Risk Assessment / Toxic Dispersion

An accident causes a toxic cloud. Questions: When does the plume reach urbanized areas? How many people are affected and where? How to distribute information quickly? Requires: real-time simulation + ad-hoc integration and synchronization of spatio-temporal data + communication through GDI.

Modeling Temporal Aspects of Geodata

A fundamental prerequisite for geosimulation in GDI is proper handling of time. Four temporal dimensions in geodata:

Temporal DimensionDefinitionSupport in GIS
Transaction time Time at which a datum gets stored, changed, or deleted in a database Typically supported by standard databases and GIS
User time Time at which a datum from the database is used in an application Typically supported
Real / valid time Time at which the observed phenomenon or event actually happened in the real world Partly supported in GIS
Scenario / branch time Future time at which a phenomenon might happen — for simulation scenarios and forecasts Not typically supported in GIS — major gap for geosimulation

Two Models for Representing Real/Valid Time

Time Stamp Model

Data changes stored at the attribute level. A new value and its timestamp are stored when an attribute changes. Efficient storage for slowly-varying attributes. Doing a time slice across all attributes requires interpolation. Requires defining what constitutes a "change" (e.g., what temperature delta triggers a new entry?). Hardly supported in current GIS.

Snapshot Model

Complete acquisition of all attribute values for one point in time. Easy and efficient access to time slices (aerial images, climate modelling). The time of change of a specific attribute cannot be detected; value changes between two snapshots are lost. Potentially very redundant and storage-intensive for mostly-static attributes. Hardly supported in current GIS.

Cooperation of GIS and Simulation Systems — Evolution

Three generations of GIS–simulation coupling:

~1988: File interfaces Manual data exchange via files. Robust and easy — still often state-of-the-art. Problems: no common method base, tedious, risk of inconsistency and information loss due to incomplete conversions and missing dimensions, no direct feedback between systems, poor 3D/4D support.
~1996: Common APIs Shared data and method access tiers. Open Programming Interfaces, 2-tier and 3-tier architectures. Common data access layer for GIS and simulation models. Problems: requires development effort; GIS still lacks 3D/4D; no standardized architecture for interoperable geosimulation.
~2004: GDI services Service-based integration via standardized GDI interfaces. Simulation systems exposing SWE interfaces (SOS, SPS); GDI consuming simulation output via standard services. Still mainly a research area.
Example — VirGIS / AtmoGIS (Bernard & Krüger, 2001): A programming library (VirGIS) extended OpenGIS specifications to handle 4D fields, offering a single interface abstracting the underlying GIS kernel. AtmoGIS built on top, providing API classes and methods for atmospheric simulation (solvers, classification, tracer calculation, visualization). This enabled the same 4D data and method base to be used by both simulation models and GIS analysis tools — a precursor to today's GDI-based approach.

Two Aspects of Geosimulation in GDI

1 — Access to time-variant geoinformation

Using time dimensions in existing GI services (WMS, WFS, WCS support optional time parameters). Integration of real-time observations via Sensor Web Enablement (SWE). Examples: IPCC Interactive Atlas, GLUES Geoportal, THREDDS Data Server for NetCDF climate data.

2 — Sharing and cooperative execution of geosimulations

Integration of geosimulation systems/services into GDI. Interoperable geosimulation services. Requires standardized interfaces for controlling, executing, and retrieving results from simulation models. Still mainly a research area.

Geo Sensor Web & Sensor Web Enablement (SWE)

Sensor Web: "A coordinated observation infrastructure composed of a distributed collection of resources — sensors, platforms, models, communications infrastructure — that can collectively behave as a single, autonomous, task-able, dynamically adaptive and reconfigurable observing system that provides raw and processed data, along with associated metadata, via a set of standards-based service-oriented interfaces. Human reports, RADAR, satellite feeds, models, and thermometers are all examples of sensors within the context of a sensor web." — OGC Sensor Web Enablement definition

Key insight: In the SWE definition, "sensor" is intentionally broad — it includes simulation models. This is the conceptual bridge between sensor data and simulation data in GDI: a simulation model can be treated as a "virtual sensor" that produces observations.

SWE Components and Their Roles

SWE ComponentRole
Sensor Observation Service (SOS) Provides access to observations and simulation results. The central data access service for sensor/simulation data — analogous to WFS for feature data but for time-series observations.
Sensor Planning Service (SPS) Parametrizes, starts, and controls a sensor or simulation run. The entry point for requesting a new observation or launching a simulation with specific parameters.
Sensor Model Language (SensorML) Describes the sensor or simulation model itself — its capabilities, parameters, coordinate reference systems, calibration history.
Observations and Measurements (O&M) Defines the conceptual model and GML encoding for observation data — how to represent a measurement with its phenomenon time, result time, observed property, feature of interest, and result value.
Sensor Event Service (SES) / Publish-Subscribe Interface Asynchronous notification — informs clients (users, applications) that new simulation results or observations are available. Essential for real-time workflows where clients cannot continuously poll.
Web Notification Service (WNS) Delivers notifications to users via various channels (email, SMS, HTTP callback).

Example: PEGELONLINE (pegelonline.wsv.de) — real-time water gauge data for German waterways via SOS interface. One of the most concrete operational SWE implementations in Germany.

Open-source implementation: 52North SOS (52north.org) and Helgoland sensor web viewer.

Integration of Geosimulation via SWE — "Simulation as Sensor"

The SWE paradigm can be applied to link simulations into GDI by treating a simulation model as a sensor:

Client registers for notifications (WNS)
→
Client sends DescribeCollection + SubmitRequest to SPS (start simulation with parameters)
→
Simulation runs, stores results to Datastore
→
SWE notifies client via Publish/Subscribe that results are ready
→
Client calls GetObservation on SOS to retrieve results
→
Results visualized via WMS or WCS

Interoperable Geosimulation — Standards Approaches

OpenMI (Open Modelling Interface)

OGC standard for coupling hydrological and environmental models. Provides interface descriptions and reference implementations. Allows models to exchange time-stepping data directly. Best suited for tightly-coupled domain-specific model chains. (ogc.org/standards/openmi)

High Level Architecture (HLA)

IEEE 1516 standard (since 2000) for distributed simulation — the most widely accepted standard in simulation engineering. Key concepts: Federates (individual simulators) organized into a Federation; a Runtime Infrastructure (RTI) manages communication and time synchronization. Strong time management; designed for interchangeability and reuse of simulation components.

GI Services + HLA Bridge: One research approach wraps an HLA-based simulation federation behind a GDI interface — a "bridge" component exposes SPS/SOS interfaces to the GDI side while communicating with the simulation federates via RTI on the simulation side. This bridges the two worlds without requiring simulation models to be rewritten.

Conclusion: Geosimulation, Sensor Web and GDI

  • Current GIS–simulation cooperation is mainly via manual file exchanges; GIS lacks good 3D and time support → tedious, information loss, no feedback loops
  • GDI standards technically support 4D, but 4D is hardly implemented in practice
  • SWE standards support integration of real-time measurements into GDI and can link simulations (simulation = sensor)
  • Community models (climate, hydrology, agriculture, ocean) allow domain-specific simulation exchange
  • Approaches toward interoperable geosimulation (HLA, OpenMI) are still primarily research — not yet mainstream GDI
6.4 · Geo Semantics

Geo Semantics & Ontologies

The Problem: Current Standards Address Syntax, Not Meaning

All the OGC standards covered so far (WMS, WFS, WCS, WPS, CSW) primarily address the syntax of exchanged geodata and operations:

  • Operation names and signatures (GetMap, GetFeature...)
  • Data types (literal, bbox, complex)
  • Communication protocols (HTTP GET/POST, SOAP, REST)

Semantics — the meaning of the data and operations — are addressed only indirectly: as common domain data models (application schemas like ATKIS, INSPIRE) or in informal textual descriptions (metadata abstracts). Machines cannot reason about textual descriptions.

A Concrete Semantic Problem — Water Gauge Data

Two WFS services, both providing water gauge observations:

BafG (Germany)CHMI (Czech Republic)
Feature type: PegelmessungFeature type: StavVody
Attribute: nameAttribute: stanice
Attribute: wasserstand_m (water level in meters)Attribute: stav (water level in centimeters)
Attribute: zeitpunktAttribute: datum
Geometry: gml:pointPropertyGeometry: gml:position

A machine reading both WFS responses would see completely different field names, different units, different GML geometry properties. It cannot automatically determine that wasserstand_m and stav describe the same concept — just in different languages and units. This is the semantic interoperability problem.

Why this matters: Syntactic interoperability (both return GML via WFS) is achieved. Schema-level interoperability (do the same attributes describe the same things?) is not. Without semantic description, automated fusion of these two datasets requires manual intervention — a human must map the fields. At GDI scale (thousands of services), manual mapping does not work.

Synonyms and Homonyms — The Core Semantic Challenge

Synonyms — same meaning, different words

Example: "Shore" and "Bank" (when looking for regions close to water) describe the same geographic concept. A system searching for "Bank" must also search for "Shore" to be semantically complete. Thesauri (chapter 5.5.1) partially address synonyms within a controlled vocabulary.

Homonyms — same word, different meanings

Example: "Bank" in geography = the land along the edge of a waterbody. "Bank" in economics = a financial institution. Without contextual knowledge, a machine cannot distinguish. This is why pure keyword search fails for geodata: "bank" in a geographic query might return financial data.

Detecting synonyms and distinguishing homonyms requires knowledge about concepts and context — not just string matching. This is what ontologies provide.

Ontologies

"An ontology is an explicit specification of a shared conceptualisation." — T. Gruber

An ontology models a knowledge domain using a standardized vocabulary, types, and relations. It formalizes the concepts of a domain, the relations between them, and the constraints that hold. — Note: this is the computer science definition, not the philosophical one. In computer science, many domain-specific ontologies coexist.

What an Ontology Contains

  • Classes — abstract concepts (Painter, Artist, River, Municipality)
  • Instances — individual objects (Picasso is an instance of Painter)
  • Properties / Relations — links between classes or instances (isPainterOf, isLocatedIn)
  • Inverse relations — paint and beingPainted are inverses; allows reasoning both ways
  • Hierarchies — Painter is a subclass of Artist; enables subsumption reasoning

Reasoning example: From the ontology hierarchy Picasso → Painter → Artist, a reasoner can automatically infer that Picasso is an Artist, even if this was never explicitly stated. Picasso and Raphael (Santi) both use the same painting technique and are both Painters — the ontology enables the system to reason about their similarity.

OWL — The Formal Language for Ontologies

OWL (Web Ontology Language) is the W3C standard for formalizing ontologies on the web. It is built on top of RDF (Resource Description Framework) — the same triple-based data model used in Linked Data (chapter 5.1). The idea: ontologies (taxonomies, relations) are formalized as OWL classes; data (instances) are described using those classes (semantic annotation); a reasoner then uses the ontology for automatic logical inference.

Key point: OWL/RDF is the technical bridge between Linked Data (chapter 5.1) and Geo Semantics. The same RDF triple structure that describes linked data resources can also express ontological class hierarchies and property definitions.

Three Applications of Ontologies in GDI

ApplicationHow ontologies helpLink to earlier chapters
1. Discovery and integration of geodata A query for "water level" at a German gauge station can be matched against a Czech WFS offering "StavVody" because an ontology maps both to the same concept. Semantic search transcends language and naming conventions. Extends catalogue services (CSW, chapter 5.5.2) and thesauri (chapter 5.5.1) — ontologies are a more powerful and formal version of thesauri
2. Data fusion Ontological reasoning helps identify which attributes in two datasets describe the same real-world property — enabling automated schema matching beyond string similarity. Similarity between concepts can be measured by their distance in the ontology hierarchy. Semantic similarity measures for geodata fusion (chapter 5.4.2)
3. Semantic geoprocessing workflow composition If every WPS process has a semantic description of its inputs and outputs (using ontology concepts), a system can automatically check whether two processes can be chained — whether the output type of process A is compatible with the input type of process B. Enables (semi-)automatic service chaining. WPS and service chaining (chapter 6.2); service chain types (chapter 5.7)

Hybrid Ontology Approach — Example for Discovery

How ontology-based discovery works (Lutz, 2005):

User query in natural language: "What is the water level at control point X at time Y in the Elbe River?"
→
Query concept is described in OWL/DL (Description Logic): formalized as "water level measurement" concept
→
Logical reasoner checks: does the application ontology of a service contain a concept equivalent to or subsumed by "water level"?
→
German BafG WFS: Pegelmessung with wasserstand_m → matched via Domain Ontology
→
Czech CHMI WFS: StavVody with stav → also matched; unit conversion applied automatically

The Hybrid Ontology approach distinguishes: a high-level Domain Ontology (general concepts like "water body", "measurement", "gauge station") and an Application Ontology (how a specific service describes its data, mapped to the domain ontology). Reasoning bridges between them.

Geo Semantics — Links to the Rest of the Lecture

Geo Semantics is the thread that runs through the entire course — it is not an isolated topic but the deep challenge underlying all interoperability:

  • Chapter 4 — Organizations: INSPIRE data specifications create common domain data models → the first step toward shared semantics
  • Chapter 5.4 — Schema Transformation: Semantic aspect of transformation (converting ATKIS → INSPIRE is fundamentally a semantic alignment problem)
  • Chapter 5.4 — Data Fusion: Semantic similarity measures use ontology reasoning to match attributes across datasets
  • Chapter 5.5 — Thesauri: Controlled vocabularies (GEMET) are simplified, non-formal ontologies — ontologies are the formal generalization
  • Chapter 6.2 — Geoprocessing: Semantic WPS profiles describe the meaning of process inputs/outputs to enable automatic workflow composition
  • Chapter 5.1 — Linked Data: RDF/OWL is the technical foundation for both Linked Data and formal ontologies — they are the same technology stack

Open Research Questions in Geo Semantics

  • How to achieve common geo-ontologies as semantic reference systems? (Who maintains them? How are they governed?)
  • How to realize automatic semantic annotation of datasets and services? (Links strongly to automated metadata generation)
  • How to best use ontologies for semantic integration of geodata at GDI scale?
  • How to achieve and use semantic descriptions of geoprocessing workflows to support (semi-)automatic service chaining?
Bottom line: Geo Semantics is not yet solved — it remains one of the most active research frontiers in GDI. Current standards achieve syntactic and structural interoperability well. Full semantic interoperability — where machines autonomously understand, discover, combine, and process geodata from unknown sources — is the grand challenge that connects all of Chapter 6 and much of the rest of the course.
